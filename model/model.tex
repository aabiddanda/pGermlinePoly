\begin{abstract}
The detection of \textit{somatic} variants from ``clonal'' sampling of tissues is a major challenge in leveraging sequencing data to understand both tissue development and cancer progression. Detecting truly somatic variants requires disentangling sequencing error and true germline polymorphism, often requiring high sequencing depth per-clone ($> 10x$ average depth). Additionally, there may be high variation in sequencing depths \textit{per clone}, which affects the power to detect a somatic variant, particularly at low variant allele frequencies (VAF). Furthermore, if a germline reference sample is provided but is not of high quality, there may be uncertainty even for high-VAF somatic variants. To address the challenge of somatic variant filtering and curation, we develop a hierarchical model leveraging multiple annotations from publicly available datasets to estimate the \textit{posterior probability of germline polymorphism} as a comparable statistic across multiple clonally sampled individuals for consistent filtering. We highlight how variation in depth of coverage amongst clones and sequencing errors can affect somatic variant detection power. Our method provides variant-level annotations for filtering across variety of somatic sequencing contexts.
\end{abstract}


\section*{Problem Motivation}

Our goal here is to develop a method to estimate the \textit{probability} that an observed clonal variant is actually somatic and not a germline variant. In actuality, we will be estimating the ``complement'', the probability that the variant is actually a germline variant. The core intuition used by a number of somatic variant callers is that (1) a germline variant is shared across \textit{all} somatic clones and (2) is also present in a germline sample \citep{Coorens2021-xy,Coorens2024-be,Spencer-Chapman2025-pj}. Without loss of generality we assume that ``clone'' refers to sequencing data from a single colony derived from a blood progenitor lineage or in cases of other tissues, a laser-capture microdissection.

We specifically want to model how the germline sample, alongside additional information can provide ``prior'' information on whether a variant is likely to be polymorphic in the germline (e.g., population allele frequency). The likelihood is then defined using the collected evidence for that polymorphism across \textit{all} of the individual clone sequences. The prior probability of germline polymorphism will be informed by specific variant-level annotations (e.g., variant-level masks, allele frequencies, region-specific mutation rate). If the hyperparameters dictating the prior distribution are estimated using Empirical Bayes, we can then robustly arrive at a posterior probability of germline polymorphism estimate per putative somatic variant.

\section*{Model}

We start from a model where the output consists of reference and alternative read counts from each of the $J$ clonal samples at the $k^{th}$ site ($R_k$ and $A_k$ respectively). Following previous modeling, we will consider that the $k^{th}$ variant falls in a category $z\in \{\texttt{somatic}, \texttt{het}\}$, where $\texttt{het}$ is a germline heterozygote or polymorphism. Considering the likelihood across 

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
P(\mathbf{A}_k, \mathbf{R_k} | z_k=\texttt{het}) &= \prod_j^J  \texttt{Bin}\left(a_{jk}, a_{jk}+r_{jk}, \frac{1}{2}\right)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}


\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
P(\mathbf{A}_k, \mathbf{R_k} | z_k=\texttt{somatic}) &= \prod_j^J  \alpha \texttt{Bin} \left(a_{jk}, a_{jk}+r_{jk}, \frac{1}{2}\right) + (1 - \alpha)\texttt{Bin}(a_{jk}, a_{jk}+r_{jk}, \epsilon)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}


Combining this likelihood definition with prior probabilities ($P(z_k = z)$) leads to a posterior probability for $P(z_k = \texttt{het} | \mathbf{A}_k, \mathbf{R_k})$:


\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
P(z_k=\texttt{het} | \mathbf{A}_k, \mathbf{R_k}) &= \frac{ P(\mathbf{A}_k, \mathbf{R_k} | z_k=\texttt{het}) \times P(z_k = \texttt{het}) }{\sum_{z \in \{\texttt{het}, \texttt{somatic}\}} P(\mathbf{A}_k, \mathbf{R_k} | z_k=z) \times P(z_k = z)   }
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}


We define the latent variable $Z_k$ as an indicator that the $k^{th}$ variant is \textit{germline polymorphic}. Following this definition,  we can also focus on using annotations $\theta_k$ to help with the "prior" probability $P(Z_{k} | \theta_{k})$ , where $\theta_{k}$ represents a set of annotations for the $k^{th}$ site (e.g., germline sample genotype likelihood, gnomAD allele frequency, local GC content, etc). We create the following logistic model for the priors based on $L$ observed annotations:

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	x_{k} &= \sum_l^L \lambda_l \theta_{kl}\\
	\pi_{k} &= P(Z_{k} = 1 | \theta_{k}, \Lambda) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-x_{k}}}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Using this kind of prior, we can model each sites genotype likelihood as coming from a mixture of the two latent distributions:

$$P(GL | \theta, \Lambda) = \prod_k^K \pi_k P(GL_k| Z_k = 1) + (1 - \pi_k)P(GL_k | Z_k = 0)$$
, where each of the $K$ sites are treated as independent realizations of this data generating process. If we empirically estimate $\hat{\alpha}_k$ as the MLE estimate of the VAF (using no prior information), then the total likelihood for the data is:



\begin{equation}
P(GL | \Lambda, \hat{\alpha}, \epsilon) = \prod^K \pi_k \left(\prod^J \sum_{g \in \{0,1,2\}} P(GL_k | P(G_j = g | Z_k = 1, \epsilon, \alpha_0) \right) + (1 - \pi_k)\left(\prod^J \sum_{g \in \{0,1,2\}} GL_k(g)P(G_j = g | Z_k = 0, \epsilon, \alpha_0) \right)
\end{equation}

Each iteration of this likelihood will take $\mathcal{O}(KJL)$ to compute. We describe some of the pre-computation and tools used for parameter estimation in a subsequent section. With parameters $\hat{\Lambda}, \hat{\alpha_0}, \epsilon$ we can estimate the posterior probability of $Z_k = 1$ as:

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
P(Z_k = 1 | GL_k, \theta_k, \Lambda) &= \frac{P(GL_k|Z_k=1)P(Z_k=1 | \Lambda, \alpha_0, \epsilon)}{P(GL_k|Z_k=1)P(Z_k=1 | \Lambda, \alpha_0,\epsilon) + P(GL_k|Z_k=0)P(Z_k=0 | \Lambda, \alpha_0, \epsilon)}\\
&=  \frac{\pi_k P(GL_k|Z_k=1, \alpha_0)}{\pi_k P(GL_k|Z_k=1, \alpha_0, \epsilon) + (1 - \pi_k)P(GL_k|Z_k=0, \alpha_0,\epsilon)}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\subsection*{Computational Methods}

\noindent Since the full likelihood takes $\mathcal{O}(KJL)$ to compute, we focus here on strategies for limiting this computation as the number of variants $K$ grows large. In realistic datasets from blood clonal sequencing available today $K \sim 10^7$ within a GATK VCF callset.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Precomputation + Thinning}

To avoid using the full dataset for hyperparameter estimation via Empirical Bayes we restrict our inference to only considering some proportion $\gamma$ of the sites in a joint-called VCF file with genotype likelihoods. Thinning to a restricted proportion of sites spread throughout the genome helps substantially for hyperparameter training.

\item \textbf{Initialization Scan}

One of the more critical parameters to be estimated is $\alpha_0$, or the concentration parameter of the Beta-Binomial distribution. The core idea here is that for true somatic variants, the alleles carried \textit{within} the pool of clones will be overdispersed relative to binomial expectations (since there is effectively a mixture of clones carrying the somatic variant).

We can use this idea in the genotype-likelihood space to estimate the parameters of a beta-binomial distribution \textit{per-site} ($\tilde{\alpha}^{(k)}_0$) to prioritize estimating the $\alpha_0$ hyperparameter genome-wide from the over-dispersed ones. The per-site likelihood is therefore:

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
	l(\alpha^{(k)}_0) &= \log P(GL_k | \alpha^{(k)}_0)\\
	&= \log \left(\prod_j^J \sum_{g\in{0,1,2}} GL_{jk}(g)P(G_{jk} = g | \alpha^{k}_0)\right) \\

\end{equation}

\section*{Limitations / Model Improvement}

\begin{itemize}
	\item Model fitting takes more time than it does for simpler linear filters.
	\item Genotype-likelihood models are slightly more opaque
	\item The model may be dependent on relevant annotations available, but many of these are known in the field.
	\item Current simulations are not from a \textit{true} somatic mutation simulator \citep{Srivatsa2023-mt}
\end{itemize}
